## 5 Training Methods

The harness analysis of §3–§4 fixes the target of weight-level training: the append-only CodeAct scaffold, whose growing-prefix trajectory is the structure gradient-based objectives are built for. This section describes what is trained, the family of training signals considered, and the prompt substrate assembled for the larger runs.

### 5.1 What is trained

The only weights updated are those of the reasoning policy — the language model that, each turn, emits a thought and a single Python action. It is fine-tuned with LoRA adapters on all linear projections, which keeps a run within a single GPU. The perceptual backbone stays frozen: the vision-language model is reached only through an external HTTP endpoint that the policy calls via `batch_look`, and no gradient flows into it. Restricting training to the reasoning policy isolates the question of interest — whether weight-level training can lift the agent's accuracy — from any change in perceptual capability.

The backbone is Qwen3.5-4B, a pivot from the Qwen3-8B model named in the original proposal. The 4B model is the one that holds the ≤8B leaderboard slot inside this agent scaffold, making it the relevant subject for a study aimed at the ≤8B tier; the larger 8B model is not the incumbent and would conflate a backbone change with the training intervention. The same model serves as both the policy under training and the policy under evaluation: trajectories are collected with the CodeAct scaffold, training updates the policy, and evaluation runs the identical scaffold, so the policy that is trained is exactly the policy that is deployed.

### 5.2 Signals explored

Three families of training signal sit on a single axis — how much information each rollout returns per unit of generation, and whether that information is on- or off-policy.

**Supervised fine-tuning / sequence-level distillation (SeqKD).** Teacher trajectories are produced by rejection sampling: many rollouts are drawn from a strong solver, and only those scoring above an ANLS threshold are kept. The policy is then fit to these accepted trajectories with the standard token-level cross-entropy loss. The signal is dense in tokens but off-policy — it returns $O(N)$ supervised tokens per trajectory, all generated by another policy. This is the cheapest signal to apply and serves as a cold-start warmup: it moves a frozen model onto the scaffold's turn structure before any on-policy stage, reducing the risk of a zero-reward-variance start in which no sampled rollout ever succeeds. The trajectories actually used for the supervised study are transfer data drawn from MMLongBench-Doc [MMLongBench-Doc, arXiv:2407.01523], a long-document multimodal benchmark that is disjoint from the DocVQA validation set and therefore leakage-free against the evaluation used in §7.

**Reinforcement learning from a verifier reward (GRPO).** Group Relative Policy Optimization [GRPO, arXiv:2402.03300] samples a group of rollouts per prompt, scores each, and uses the within-group reward spread as a baseline-free advantage. The reward is the answer-level ANLS of the submitted answer against the gold answer, augmented with shaping terms for tool use and code execution — for instance, penalizing malformed actions that fail to parse or execute. The signal is on-policy but sparse: each rollout returns a single scalar, $O(1)$ of information regardless of trajectory length, paid for by generating the full multi-turn rollout.

**On-policy distillation (OPD).** The policy generates its own rollouts, and a per-token KL divergence to the frozen 27B teacher supplies a dense correction at every position — the on-policy, dense-feedback counterpart to the sparse scalar of RL [GKD, arXiv:2306.13649]. The signal returns $O(N)$ per-token feedback that is on-policy, combining RL's distribution match with SeqKD's token density. Because the gradient is a per-token KL to a teacher rather than a clipped surrogate over a scalar reward, it is less prone to the catastrophic forgetting and high-variance updates of reward-only RL.

These signals are not mutually exclusive. The advantages from a verifier reward and from a per-token teacher KL can be summed into a single update, letting one stage carry both the sparse answer-level signal and the dense per-token correction.

The cost framing — SeqKD as $O(N)$ off-policy tokens, RL as $O(1)$ on-policy scalar, OPD as $O(N)$ on-policy per-token feedback — orders the signals by information per rollout and by distribution match, and motivates using the cheap off-policy stage for warmup and the on-policy stages for the lift.

### 5.3 Training-data pool

The supervised study of §7 draws its trajectories from the transfer set above. A separate, larger prompt substrate was assembled afterward and has not yet been used for any training run reported here; it is the intended substrate for an upcoming larger-data supervised run and for the RL and OPD runs, not a source of any §7 number.

The pool is method-agnostic — a collection of document-question prompts usable by any of the signals in §5.2 — and draws from seven DocVQA-family datasets: DocVQA [DocVQA, arXiv:2007.00398], InfographicVQA [InfographicVQA, arXiv:2104.12756], ChartQA [ChartQA, arXiv:2203.10244], MapQA [MapQA, arXiv:2211.08545], MP-DocVQA [MP-DocVQA, arXiv:2212.05935], TAT-DQA [TAT-DQA, arXiv:2207.11871], and SlideVQA [SlideVQA, arXiv:2301.04883]. These span single- and multi-page documents, infographics, charts, maps, and slide decks, covering the visual and structural variety the DocVQA-2026 categories present.

Two constraints shape the pool. A weighted per-source sampler controls the contribution of each dataset rather than pooling raw question counts, which would let the largest source — MapQA — dominate the mixture; the weighting de-dominates MapQA and balances the substrate across document types. The pool is also constructed to be leakage-safe with respect to the DocVQA validation set, so that any model trained on it can be evaluated on that validation set without contamination.
